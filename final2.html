Risk Control

Managing and reducing potential threats to an organization

  • Risk control is a key part of risk management
  • It focuses on reducing impact and likelihood of risks
  • Used in cybersecurity, business, finance, and operations
  • Helps organizations make smart decisions

Risk Control Strategies

  • Defend – Protect systems using security controls
  • Transfer – Shift risk to another party (e.g., insurance)
  • Mitigate – Reduce risk impact or likelihood
  • Accept – Acknowledge risk without action
  • Terminate – Remove the source of risk completely

These strategies help organizations choose the best response depending on risk severity.

Defend Strategy

  • Use firewalls, antivirus, encryption
  • Implement strong authentication systems
  • Monitor systems continuously
  • Train employees on cybersecurity awareness
  • Goal: prevent attacks before they happen

Transfer Strategy

  • Use cyber insurance
  • Outsource risky operations
  • Create contracts with liability clauses
  • Shared responsibility with vendors
  • Goal: shift financial burden

Mitigate Strategy

  • Reduce risk probability
  • Implement backup systems
  • Create disaster recovery plans
  • Limit access to sensitive data
  • Goal: minimize damage

Accept Strategy

  • Used when risk is low or unavoidable
  • No immediate action taken
  • Monitor the risk over time
  • Prepare contingency plans
  • Goal: tolerate manageable risks

Terminate Strategy

  • Stop risky activity completely
  • Remove vulnerable systems
  • Avoid high-risk business processes
  • Used for severe threats
  • Goal: eliminate risk entirely

Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)

  • Compares costs vs benefits
  • Helps in decision-making
  • Used before implementing controls

Costs

  • Technology expenses
  • Training and manpower
  • Maintenance

Benefits

  • Reduced losses
  • Better security
  • Improved reputation

Decision: Implement if benefits outweigh costs.

Recitation: "To Insure or Not to Insure?"

Pro (Yes to Insurance)

  • Covers financial losses
  • Helps recovery after cyberattacks
  • Provides expert support

Con (No to Insurance)

  • Can be expensive
  • Not all damages are covered
  • May reduce focus on prevention
