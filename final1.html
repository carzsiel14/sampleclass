Topic 1 Risk assessment

What is Risk Assessment?

It is a structured process used to identify what can go wrong, estimate how likely it is, evaluate how serious the consequences might be, and rank which risks deserve action first.

Core purpose

Risk assessment answers two big questions: “How likely is the event?” and “How much damage will it cause?”

Likelihood: chance of occurrence Impact: size of consequence Priority: which risk to treat first
! Without assessment, organizations may spend time on small issues while major threats remain unmanaged.
1

Identify the risk

Name the threat, hazard, or unwanted event that could affect the project, system, or organization.

2

Estimate likelihood

Judge how often or how easily the event may happen based on evidence, experience, or data.

3

Evaluate impact

Determine the possible effect on safety, money, operations, reputation, or compliance.

4

Prioritize response

Place the most serious risks at the top so controls, budgets, and actions can be focused properly.

Goal: make better decisions by comparing risks in a consistent way.

Topic 1 Comparison

Qualitative vs Quantitative Assessment

Both approaches evaluate risk, but they differ in the way evidence is expressed and in the level of precision they provide.

Approach A

Qualitative Assessment

Ql
  • •Uses descriptive categories such as low, medium, and high.
  • •Commonly based on expert judgment, interviews, observations, and experience.
  • •Useful when data is limited or when a quick decision is needed.
  • •Easy to explain, but it can be more subjective.

Output

Words or rating labels

Speed

Fast and simple

Best use

Limited data, early review

Approach B

Quantitative Assessment

Qt
  • •Uses numerical values such as probabilities, frequencies, costs, or score ranges.
  • •Often relies on historical records, statistics, and measurable evidence.
  • •Useful when precision and comparison are important for planning or budgeting.
  • •More objective, but it usually needs more time and more data.

Output

Numbers and calculations

Speed

Slower but more exact

Best use

Detailed analysis and ranking

Simple rule: qualitative tells you the level of risk; quantitative tells you the value of risk.

Topic 2 Formula

Calculating Risk

A common classroom method is to assign a score to likelihood and a score to impact, then multiply them to obtain a risk value.

Core formula

Risk = Likelihood × Impact

The higher the likelihood and the greater the impact, the higher the final risk score. A 1–5 scale is commonly used, but organizations may adjust the scale to fit their own policy.

1. Rate likelihood

Decide how probable the event is, for example from 1 = rare to 5 = almost certain.

2. Rate impact

Judge the seriousness of the effect, for example from 1 = minor to 5 = severe.

3. Multiply the scores

The result becomes the risk score, which can then be compared with other risks on the same scale.

Worked example

System outage during peak operations

If the likelihood is rated 4 and the impact is rated 5, then the risk score is:

4 × 5 = 20

A score of 20 would normally be treated as a very high or critical risk.

Why the formula helps

  • Creates a consistent way to compare many different risks.
  • Supports prioritization, reporting, and control decisions.
  • Makes it easier to explain why one issue needs faster action than another.
1–5 Low 6–10 Moderate 11–15 High 16–25 Critical
Remember: a risk score is a decision tool, not just a number.
