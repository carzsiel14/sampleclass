Why Information Assurance Matters

KEY POINTS
  • Information assets: student data, records, finances, systems
  • Consequences: money loss, downtime, damaged reputation, legal issues
  • Security is socio-technical: people + process + technology
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
Schools are real targets because they store identity data, grades, accounts, and sometimes payment info. One weak password can lead to unauthorized access; one ransomware incident can stop enrollment or classes. This is why we study IA: not only to stop hackers, but to keep services running and protect people‚Äôs data.

Key Concepts: IA, InfoSec, Cybersecurity

KEY POINTS
  • Information Assurance (IA): trustworthiness, resilience, governance + risk
  • Information Security (InfoSec): protects information from unauthorized access/changes
  • Cybersecurity: protects digital systems and networks from cyber attacks
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
A simple way to remember: InfoSec focuses on protecting the data. Cybersecurity focuses on defending systems online. Information Assurance is broader‚Äîit includes policies, risk decisions, continuity plans, and ensuring the system stays trustworthy over time.

IA vs InfoSec vs Cybersecurity (Example)

DISCUSSION EXAMPLE
Scenario: A school portal stores grades and student profiles.

InfoSec asks: Are the grades protected from unauthorized viewing or changes?
Cybersecurity asks: Can attackers break into the portal through the network or web vulnerabilities?
IA asks: Do we have policies, backups, monitoring, access review, and incident response so the portal remains trustworthy and available?
CHECK FOR UNDERSTANDING
  • Which area covers backups and recovery planning?
  • Which area focuses on network defense?

SECURITY OBJECTIVES

CIA triad and other goals

The CIA Triad

Confidentiality
Only authorized people can access information.
Examples: access control, encryption, MFA
Integrity
Information stays accurate and unmodified.
Examples: hashing, checksums, change control
Availability
Systems and data are accessible when needed.
Examples: backups, redundancy, fault tolerance
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
The CIA triad is the most common security model. Most controls you‚Äôll encounter are designed to protect one or more of these. When you evaluate a system, always ask: ‚ÄúWhat can harm confidentiality? integrity? availability?‚Äù

CIA Examples (Campus Context)

KEY POINTS
  • Confidentiality: leaked grades, stolen student IDs, exposed passwords
  • Integrity: unauthorized grade changes, altered records
  • Availability: portal down during enrollment, ransomware locking files
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
A good security mindset is to connect theory to real life. In a school environment, even ‚Äúsmall‚Äù issues (like sharing passwords) can lead to serious confidentiality and integrity violations.

Beyond CIA

KEY POINTS
  • Authentication: proving identity (passwords, biometrics, MFA)
  • Authorization: giving correct permissions (roles, access levels)
  • Non-repudiation: actions can‚Äôt be denied later (logs, digital signatures)
  • Accountability: tracing actions to individuals (audit trails)
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
Many security failures happen because identity and access are weak. Even if encryption exists, the wrong user having the wrong permission is still a breach.

CONTROLS & TRADEOFFS

How protection works in organizations

Security Controls

KEY POINTS
  • Administrative: policies, training, procedures, risk management
  • Technical: firewalls, MFA, encryption, antivirus, IDS/IPS
  • Physical: locks, CCTV, guards, secure rooms
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
Good security isn‚Äôt only ‚Äútools.‚Äù Policies and training matter because people make mistakes. Physical controls matter because access to a computer can bypass many digital protections.

Security Tradeoffs

KEY POINTS
  • Security vs Usability: strong rules may be inconvenient
  • Security vs Cost: tools + staff + training cost money
  • Security vs Performance: controls can slow systems
  • Goal: balance controls with real risks
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
Security decisions are business decisions too. ‚ÄúPerfect security‚Äù is impossible. We reduce risk to an acceptable level by choosing the right controls for the most likely and most damaging threats.

Threats, Vulnerabilities, Risk

KEY POINTS
  • Threat: something that can cause harm (actor/event)
  • Vulnerability: weakness that can be exploited
  • Risk: likelihood √ó impact
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
Example: ‚ÄúPhisher‚Äù is a threat actor. ‚ÄúUser reuses password‚Äù is a vulnerability. Risk depends on how likely the phishing is and how bad the impact is (e.g., portal takeover).

CULTURE & ETHICS

Security is also about behavior

Security Culture

KEY POINTS
  • Security is everyone‚Äôs responsibility
  • Human error is a major attack path
  • Least privilege and need-to-know
  • Report suspicious activity early
  • Update and back up systems regularly
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
Many attacks succeed because of behavior: clicking links, sharing passwords, ignoring updates. A strong culture reduces those risks.

Ethics & Professional Responsibility

KEY POINTS
  • Use security tools only with permission and within lab scope
  • Respect privacy and confidentiality
  • Follow laws, school rules, and policies
  • Document actions honestly
DISCUSSION / INSTRUCTOR NOTES
In this course, we study attacks to understand defense. That means we must be strict about scope. Unauthorized scanning or phishing on real systems is not ‚Äúpractice‚Äù‚Äîit‚Äôs misconduct.

Lecture Wrap-Up

TAKEAWAYS
  • IA ensures trustworthiness and resilience of information
  • CIA triad guides most security decisions
  • Controls: administrative, technical, physical
  • Tradeoffs must be managed
  • Culture and ethics are foundational

LAB EXERCISE 1

Baseline Workstation Security Audit

Exercise 1 Objectives

  • Assess security posture using a checklist
  • Check accounts, updates, firewall, AV, backups
  • Map findings to CIA triad
  • Write recommendations like a real auditor

Part A: Prepare Your Sandbox

  1. Install VirtualBox/VMware (if needed)
  2. Import/Create your assigned VM
  3. Create snapshot: IAS1-Week1-Baseline
  4. Confirm internet access (NAT)
  5. Create a folder for screenshots and notes
The snapshot is your safety net. If you make a mistake, you can roll back and continue without losing progress.

Part B: Account & Password Check

STEPS
  • Open Users/Accounts settings
  • List accounts; identify admin/root accounts
  • Check password policy (length, complexity, expiration)
  • Enable MFA where available (demo)
EVIDENCE TO RECORD
  • Screenshot of account list
  • Notes on default/weak accounts
  • Password policy summary

Part C: Patch & Update Status

STEPS
  • Open Windows Update / package manager
  • Check last update date
  • Install security updates (if allowed)
  • Reboot if required
Updates matter because most attacks reuse known vulnerabilities. Patch management is one of the best ‚Äúbasic‚Äù defenses.

Part D: Protection Settings

  • Check antivirus/endpoint protection status
  • Confirm firewall is ON
  • Review screen lock & timeout
  • Check disk encryption status (if available)
This is where you often find ‚Äúeasy wins‚Äù like a disabled firewall or no screen lock.

Part E: Storage & Backup Check

  • Verify disk space
  • Locate backup settings (File History/TimeShift)
  • Confirm backup schedule or manual backup option
Availability isn‚Äôt only ‚Äúserver uptime.‚Äù Backups are how you recover after mistakes, failures, or ransomware.

Part F: Map Findings to CIA

  • For each issue, identify CIA impact:
  • Confidentiality, Integrity, and/or Availability
  • Example: Disabled firewall ‚Üí Availability + Confidentiality risk
