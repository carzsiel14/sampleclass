Social Engineering

Manipulation of people to gain access, money, or sensitive data. The goal is to trigger emotion and bypass judgment.

  • Authority, urgency, fear, curiosity, or sympathy
  • Pretexting: a believable story or role
  • Impersonation: vendor, coworker, or IT support

Common Objectives

  • Credential theft or MFA fatigue approval
  • Wire transfer or gift card fraud
  • Malware delivery and persistence

Attackers want a fast "yes".

Attack Lifecycle

  1. Recon: collect names, roles, vendors, habits.
  2. Pretext: craft a believable story.
  3. Delivery: email, phone, text, or chat.
  4. Exploit: click, login, payment, or data.
  5. Exit: cover tracks and pivot.

Psychology Triggers

  • Urgency: "within 30 minutes"
  • Authority: "CFO request"
  • Fear: "account suspended"
  • Curiosity: "see the document"
  • Reciprocity: "you owe me a favor"
  • Sympathy: "urgent personal issue"

Defense: pause and verify.

Phishing (Email)

Looks official, often includes links or attachments.

  • Display name mismatch or spoofed domain
  • Login page that imitates a real service
  • Attachment types: .zip, .html, .iso

Safe move: use a known bookmark.

Vishing (Voice)

Callers use spoofed numbers and pressure.

  • Urgent account or payroll changes
  • Asks for MFA codes or passwords
  • Claims to be IT or a vendor

Safe move: hang up, call back.

Smishing (SMS)

Short texts with shortened links.

  • Delivery failed or account alert
  • Short URL or random domain
  • Typos or unexpected timing

Safe move: open the app directly.

Phishing Red Flags

  • Unexpected urgency or secrecy
  • Sender domain slightly off or display mismatch
  • Generic greeting or missing context
  • Unusual payment, gift card, or login request
  • Links that hide real destinations
  • Unexpected attachments or file types

Safe Response

Pause and verify using a trusted channel, not by replying to the message itself. Report suspicious items immediately.

  • Hover links or inspect URLs
  • Use a known contact directory
  • Report to the security or help desk team
  • If you clicked, disconnect and report fast

Insider Threats

Risk from someone with legitimate access. Not always malicious; mistakes and compromised accounts count.

  • Careless: mis-sent files, weak passwords
  • Compromised: stolen credentials, MFA fatigue
  • Malicious: data theft, sabotage, fraud

Motivations and Risk Factors

  • Financial pressure or personal stress
  • Grievances or policy conflicts
  • Excess access without oversight
  • Privilege creep over time

Goal: detect early, respond calmly.

Behavioral Signals

  • Unusual access times or volumes
  • Repeated policy bypass attempts
  • Downloads outside job role
  • Sudden resignation with data spikes
  • Large transfers to personal storage

Risk Reduction Controls

  • Least privilege and access reviews
  • Separation of duties for payments
  • Offboarding checklist and access removal
  • Logging, DLP, and anomaly alerts
  • Culture that encourages reporting

Security is a team sport.

Human-Centered Defenses

Build habits that slow down attackers and protect data.

  • Awareness training and simulations
  • Least privilege and strong approvals
  • MFA and phishing-resistant methods
  • Clear reporting pathways
  • Security champions in each team

Verify Script

"I need to verify this request."

  • Confirm the sender via known contact
  • Use internal ticketing for approvals
  • Document what you saw and when

Use STOP: Stop, Think, Observe, Proceed.

Phish Response Playbook

  1. Stop: do not click or reply.
  2. Capture: screenshot or save the email.
  3. Verify: call the requester via known channel.
  4. Report: forward to security or help desk.
  5. Recover: reset passwords if you clicked.

Do Not

  • Do not use links or numbers in the message.
  • Do not forward to coworkers for opinions.
  • Do not share MFA codes or passwords.
  • Do not delay reporting; minutes matter.

Fast reporting reduces impact.

Wrap-Up

  • Attackers target people first, systems second.
  • Pause, verify, and report quickly.
  • Insider risk is everyone's responsibility.
  • One habit beats a hundred warnings.
